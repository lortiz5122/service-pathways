# prep-pathway.md — Pre-Enlistment / Pre-Commissioning Preparation Pathway

*A neutral factual reference for prospective service members aged 16–24 and their parents/counselors. Current as of retrieval date 2026-07-12. Figures carry a named source and retrieval date; unconfirmed items are marked UNVERIFIED. This is not recruiting material.*

---

## TL;DR
- **The ASVAB gates everything twice:** your AFQT percentile decides *whether* you can enlist (branch minimums run 31–36 for diploma holders, ~50 for GED holders), while separate line/composite scores decide *which* job you can hold — a high AFQT alone never guarantees a desired MOS/rating/AFSC.
- **Fitness standards are in active flux across all six branches in 2025–2026:** the Army replaced the ACFT with the five-event Army Fitness Test (AFT) with sex-neutral combat standards; the Navy, Air Force, and Space Force all moved to twice-yearly testing; the Marine Corps added sex-neutral combat-arms PFT scoring; and the Coast Guard is standing up a formal PFT for 2026. Verify current standards before relying on any number.
- **The realistic path from first recruiter contact to shipping out is weeks to many months**, routed through MEPS (a 1–2 day medical/aptitude/contract process) and usually the Delayed Entry Program (DEP, up to 365 days). Get the job guarantee in writing in the enlistment contract; a recruiter's verbal promise is not binding.

## 1. ASVAB Prep Strategy

### What the ASVAB is and current formats
The Armed Services Vocational Aptitude Battery (ASVAB) is an aptitude test measuring readiness for military and civilian occupations, not an IQ test. Per officialasvab.com (retrieved 2026-07-12), it is delivered in three forms:

| Format | Where | Questions | Adaptive? | Verification | Retake |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **CAT-ASVAB** (computer adaptive) | MEPS | ~135 | Yes; can't change prior answers | None | 1 month to 2nd try |
| **P&P-ASVAB** (paper) | MET sites, high schools | 225 | No; can skip/return within a subtest | None | 1 month to 2nd try |
| **PiCAT** (unproctored, at-home) | Anywhere online | ~145 | Yes | Requires in-person Vtest at MEPS within 45 days | Cannot be retaken; only for those who never took ASVAB |

Per officialasvab.com (retrieved 2026-07-12): the PiCAT access code (from a recruiter) expires 30 days after issue; once started, it must be finished within 48 hours; the proctored CAT-ASVAB averages about 2 hours; the Vtest takes 25–30 minutes and yields no separate score — it only validates the PiCAT. If Vtest performance is inconsistent, the applicant takes the full ASVAB and that score governs.

### The ten subtests
Per officialasvab.com and military.com (retrieved 2026-07-12): General Science (GS), Arithmetic Reasoning (AR), Word Knowledge (WK), Paragraph Comprehension (PC), Mathematics Knowledge (MK), Electronics Information (EI), Auto Information (AI) and Shop Information (SI) — split on CAT-ASVAB, combined on paper — Mechanical Comprehension (MC), and Assembling Objects (AO).

### AFQT computation vs. line scores
The **Armed Forces Qualification Test (AFQT)** is not a separate test but a percentile (1–99) derived from four subtests using the formula **AFQT = 2×VE + AR + MK**, where VE (Verbal Expression) is an optimally weighted WK+PC composite (source: military.com and asvabhero.com, retrieved 2026-07-12). A 50 means you scored as well as or better than 50% of a national reference sample. DoD categories (source: cogn-iq.org citing DoD norming, retrieved 2026-07-12): I (93–99), II (65–92), IIIA (50–64), IIIB (31–49), IV (10–30), V (1–9). By law, Category V cannot enlist and Category IV is capped at a small share of accessions.

The AFQT is the **enlistment gate**. Separate **line/composite scores** (built from all ten subtests) are the **job gate** — this is why a high AFQT alone doesn't guarantee a desired MOS/rating/AFSC: each specialty sets its own composite minimum, and you also need an open slot, security-clearance eligibility, and medical/physical qualification.

### Minimum AFQT by branch (Tier 1 diploma vs Tier 2 GED)
Resolved after targeted research (retrieved 2026-07-12):

| Branch | Tier 1 (diploma) | Tier 2 (GED) | Source / note |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Army** | 31 | 50 | goarmy.com (official). Future Soldier Prep Course exists for 21–49 scorers. |
| **Navy** | 31 | 50 | navycs.com citing COMNAVCRUITCOMINST 1130.8. The widely-cited "35" is outdated — do not use as current. |
| **Air Force** | 31 (per airforce.com/asvab) — but 36 commonly cited operationally | 50 (official page) / 65 (operational) | **CONFLICT:** airforce.com's ASVAB page says 31/50; its how-to-join page and recruiting affiliates say 36/65. Flagged. |
| **Space Force** | No branch-specific number published; inherits Air Force pipeline/figures | Same as Air Force | spaceforce.com states only "a qualifying ASVAB score." Highly selective in practice. |
| **Marine Corps** | 31 (marines.com) — many secondary sources say 32 | 50 | Official marines.com says 31; "32" is widely repeated. GED capped ~5% of accessions. |
| **Coast Guard** | 32 | 50 (secondary sources) | Per GAO-25-107224, the November 2023 update to the Coast Guard Recruiting instruction reduced the minimum AFQT for diploma holders "to 32 from 36" (previously 40) and raised the maximum enlistment age to 41 from 34; the Coast Guard reported the average score for active-duty enlisted recruits in 2024 was 64. Reserve minimum reportedly still 40 (UNVERIFIED). |

GED (Tier 2) applicants are capped at a small percentage of accessions in every branch and generally need AFQT 50; earning ~15 college credits reclassifies a GED holder as Tier 1, dropping them to the lower diploma minimum. For scale: the Coast Guard enlisted 5,204 members in FY2025, its highest since 1991, for a force of roughly 42,000 active-duty, with new-enlisted starting salary around $28,000/year (per asvabhero.com and GAO, 2026).

### Line/composite systems by branch (the job gate)
Source: liveabout.com, asvabhero.com, achievable.me (retrieved 2026-07-12):
- **Army** — 10 composites: GT (General Technical = VE+AR), CL (Clerical), EL (Electronics), MM (Mechanical Maintenance), GM, FA, OF, SC, ST (Skilled Technical), CO. GT gates intelligence, Special Forces selection, and officer programs. Example: 35F Intelligence Analyst needs ST 101; 25S SATCOM Operator needs EL 117.
- **Air Force & Space Force** — **MAGE** composites (percentiles): **M** = AR + 2(VE) + MC + AS; **A** = VE + MK; **G** = VE + AR; **E** = AR + MK + EI + GS. Each AFSC/SFSC sets minimums (e.g., Pararescue G ≈ 44 with M 60; Cyber Systems needs high G/E). Note: older sites list an obsolete A = NO+CS+VE formula; those subtests were removed decades ago.
- **Marine Corps** — three composites: GT (VE+AR), EL (GS+AR+MK+EI), MM (GS+AS+MK+EI). GT still gates infantry/combat MOSs.
- **Navy & Coast Guard** — no line scores; they sum raw subtest standard scores per rating (e.g., a rating may require "VE+AR+MK+MC ≥ X"). Navy Nuclear Field is among the highest composites.

### Study strategy and retake policy
- **Free official resources (retrieved 2026-07-12):** officialasvab.com (test info, practice), March2Success (Army-sponsored study site), and the ASVAB Career Exploration Program (ASVAB CEP / asvabprogram.com) used in high schools.
- **Retake policy (source: airforce.com and military.com, retrieved 2026-07-12):** wait **1 month** after the first test to retest, **another 1 month** for a third attempt, then **6 months** before any further retakes. A retake **replaces** (does not supplement) the prior score — the most recent test is the score of record, even if lower. Scores are valid **2 years**. The PiCAT itself cannot be retaken.

## 2. Physical Fitness Standards by Branch

**Distinguish two different things:** (a) **shipping/accession fitness** — what you must do at boot camp/entry, and (b) **in-service annual (or now often semi-annual) fitness tests** — the recurring test of record. The tables below emphasize the in-service test of record because that is what "the standard" usually refers to; entry expectations are lower but exist.

### Army — Army Fitness Test (AFT)
Source: army.mil/aft and army.mil/article/284799 (retrieved 2026-07-12). Effective **June 1, 2025**, the AFT replaced the Army Combat Fitness Test (ACFT). The Standing Power Throw was removed, leaving **five events**: 3-Rep-Max Deadlift (MDL), Hand-Release Push-Up (HRP), Sprint-Drag-Carry (SDC), Plank (PLK), and Two-Mile Run (2MR). Scoring: 0–100 points/event, 60 minimum per event, 500 max.
- **General standard:** ≥60/event and **≥300 total**, age- and sex-normed.
- **Combat standard (21 designated combat MOS/AOCs, e.g., 11B Infantry, Armor, Special Forces, Combat Engineer):** ≥60/event and **≥350 total**, **sex-neutral**, age-normed.
- Implementation: combat standards enforceable **Jan 1, 2026** (active) and **June 1, 2026** (Guard/Reserve). Active-duty tests twice/year; Guard/Reserve once/year. Representative combat-standard minimums for ages 17–21 (source: aftcalculatorscore.com summarizing official tables, retrieved 2026-07-12): 150 lb deadlift, 15 HRP, 2:28 SDC, 1:30 plank, 19:57 two-mile run. A separate seven-event **Combat Field Test (CFT)** is being introduced for combat specialties (UNVERIFIED as fully fielded).

### Navy — Physical Fitness Assessment (PFA)
Source: OPNAVINST 6110.1L (29 Dec 2025), NAVADMIN 242/24, USNI News and Navy Times (retrieved 2026-07-12). The PFA = **Body Composition Assessment (BCA)** + **Physical Readiness Test (PRT)**. PRT modalities: push-ups, forearm plank, and a 1.5-mile run (alternates: stationary bike, treadmill, Concept-2 row, or 500-yd/450-m swim at CO discretion).
- **Major 2026 change:** the Navy moved from **one** to **two** PFA cycles per year (Jan–Jun and Jul–Dec).
- **Failure policy tightened:** three PFA failures within four years now triggers administrative separation (previously two consecutive).
- **BCA/high-performance exception:** a Sailor exceeding BCA but scoring high enough on the PRT can still pass; for 2026 the threshold tightened to an Outstanding-Low overall with Excellent-Medium or better in all three PRT categories.
- Combat-arms billets take one PFA and one **Combat Fitness Assessment** (BCA + combat fitness test: 800m swim with fins, push-ups, pull-ups, and a 1-mile run weighted with 20 lb).

### Air Force — Physical Fitness Assessment (PFA)
Source: DAFMAN 36-2905, af.mil, airandspaceforces.com (retrieved 2026-07-12). Components: cardio, upper-body (push), core, and body composition. **Alternate components** exist: cardio via **1.5-mile run** or **20-meter HAMR shuttle** (or 2-km walk for medical profiles); push via traditional or **hand-release push-ups**; core via **sit-ups, cross-leg reverse crunch, or forearm plank**.
- **Body composition** uses **Waist-to-Height Ratio (WHtR)**, a pass/fail component (≤0.55 to pass; ≤0.46 for full points), replacing the old tape test.
- **2026 overhaul:** official scoring under the updated model activates September 1, 2026 — the **two-mile run replaces the 1.5-mile run** as the primary cardio event and testing becomes **twice yearly**. Rebalanced points (source: afptcalculator.com summarizing DAFMAN update dated 2025-09-23): cardio 50, body composition 20, strength 15, core 15; pass ≥75, Excellent ≥90. Alternate-component availability is being narrowed at BMT. Verify current phase before relying on either the old or new scoring.

### Marine Corps — PFT and CFT
Source: fitness.marines.mil, MCO 6100.13A, MARADMIN 613/25, DVIDS (retrieved 2026-07-12). Two separate tests, each 300-point max, each requiring ≥40/event and 135 to pass (First Class ≈ 225–235+).
- **PFT** (Jan 1–Jun 30): pull-ups **or** push-ups (push-ups cap at 70 pts), plank, 3-mile run (5-km row alternate for age 46+ or medical).
- **CFT** (Jul 1–Dec 31): Movement to Contact (880-yd sprint), Ammunition Lift (30-lb can, 2 min), Maneuver Under Fire (300-yd combat course).
- **2026 change:** per MARADMIN 613/25 (effective Jan 1, 2026), combat-arms Marines take a **sex-neutral PFT** scored on the male age-normed scale, needing **≥210 points (70%)**. Non-combat-arms Marines keep sex- and age-normed scoring. Full systems implementation projected within a year.

### Space Force — Human Performance Assessment (HPA)
Source: SPFMAN 36-2905 (26 Sep 2025), spaceforce.mil, airandspaceforces.com, clearancejobs.com (retrieved 2026-07-12). The Space Force initially replaced the annual PT test with a **Holistic Health Approach (HHA)** and a voluntary wearable-device **Continuous Fitness Assessment (CFA)** study begun May 2023 (now in phase "CFA PRIME"). **It has since revised course:** SPFMAN 36-2905 established the service's first fitness-for-duty standards, and per a December 8, 2025 interim change plus January 6, 2026 guidance, **all Guardians take two physical fitness tests per year beginning January 1, 2026**. The HPA mirrors the new Air Force test: cardio (2-mile run or 20m HAMR; at least one of the two annual tests must be the run), strength (tempo or hand-release push-ups), and endurance (sit-ups, cross-leg reverse crunch, or plank), plus WHtR body composition. The Space Force program is deliberately narrower and newer than the other services' — not padded here.

### Coast Guard — Physical Fitness Test (PFT)
Source: gocoastguard.com, dcms.uscg.mil (Boat Crew Fitness Test), mybaseguide.com, military.com (retrieved 2026-07-12). Because the Coast Guard sits under **DHS, not DoD**, its fitness program is administered separately. Recruits must pass an entrance PFT in week one of the 8-week Cape May boot camp; failure can mean recycling or separation. The recruit assessment historically includes push-ups, sit-ups (planks in updated standards), a 1.5-mile run (times roughly 10:30–13:30 by age/sex), and swim/tread-water tasks; recruit body-fat caps are commonly cited at 22% (men)/33% (women) (UNVERIFIED as current official). The Coast Guard introduced a **Physical Readiness Program (PRP)** with a formal service-wide **PFT effective July 1, 2026** using Boat Crew standards (push-ups, timed forearm plank, and a choice of 1.5-mile run, 12-minute swim, or 2000-m row), preceded by an anonymous baseline PFA in 2025.

### Height/weight, body composition, tattoos, medical, vision/hearing (high level)
- Every branch enforces height/weight tables with a body-fat backstop (tape or WHtR); exceeding weight triggers a body-composition measurement. Standards vary by branch, age, and sex — do not treat one branch's number as universal.
- **Tattoos:** all branches restrict tattoos on the head, face, and (usually) hands/neck; sleeve and content rules vary and have generally been loosening to aid recruiting (UNVERIFIED per-branch specifics as of 2026-07-12).
- **Medical:** screened at MEPS against DoDI 6130.03; disqualifying conditions may be waiverable (see §5).
- **Vision/hearing:** MEPS conducts full color-perception, acuity, and audiometric screening; corrective lenses are generally allowed, but some specialties (aviation, special operations, nuclear) impose stricter uncorrected/color standards.

## 3. Recruiter Process Timeline

### Step-by-step
Source: goarmy.com/how-to-join/steps, military.com, navycs.com, operationmilitarykids.org (retrieved 2026-07-12):
1. **Initial contact** with a recruiter (15–30 min overview).
2. **Recruiter pre-screening** — marital status, health history, education, drug use, arrest record; medical pre-screen form reviewed by the MEPS Chief Medical Officer before you can process (medical read normally ≤3 working days unless records are requested).
3. **ASVAB** — at a MEPS or MET site, or via PiCAT.
4. **MEPS** — medical exam, aptitude verification, job counseling, contract, oath (details below).
5. **Job/specialty selection** — with a service liaison/guidance counselor.
6. **Contract signing + Oath of Enlistment.**
7. **DEP** — most enlistees wait here until a ship date.
8. **Ship to Basic Training.**

### What happens at MEPS
Source: military.com, mepcom-referenced navycs.com, practicetestgeeks.com, indeed.com (retrieved 2026-07-12). There are **65 MEPS** nationwide. Processing is typically **1–2 days**; applicants often stay in a government-paid hotel the night before with a ~04:00 wake-up and ~05:00 report. The day includes: check-in and fingerprinting, ASVAB (if not already taken), a head-to-toe medical exam (vision including color perception, hearing/audiogram, blood pressure, blood and urine labs, drug/alcohol test, orthopedic range-of-motion "duckwalk," and for women a pregnancy test with a female attendant), a pre-enlistment interview, job selection with a counselor, contract signing, and the Oath of Enlistment. **Bring:** Social Security card, photo ID, birth certificate (long form), and all relevant medical/legal records. The MEPS physical is valid **2 years**; DEP enlistees do a shortened re-verification on ship day.

### How specialty selection actually works
Jobs are assigned by the intersection of **needs of the service × applicant qualification × available slots**. Your ASVAB line/composite scores qualify or disqualify you; security-clearance eligibility (background, citizenship, finances) gates intelligence/cyber/nuclear jobs; and a slot must be open in the shipping window you want. The Air Force/Space Force often use a "dream sheet" preference list rather than a guaranteed job at signing. **A high AFQT does not guarantee a specific job.**

### Realistic timeline
Source: collegevine.com (retrieved 2026-07-12) and DEP rules below: from first contact to ship date commonly runs **~1–3 months on the short end but frequently much longer** — up to the full 365-day DEP limit — depending on job availability, school graduation, medical waivers, and clearance. Medical or legal review is the most common delay.

### Working with a recruiter; what's binding
A recruiter's job performance is measured by accessions, so interests can diverge from yours. **What a recruiter can do:** schedule testing/MEPS, explain programs, help assemble waiver documentation. **What a recruiter cannot promise:** a job, bonus, duty station, or ship date that is not written into the enlistment contract (DD Form 4) and its annexes. **Get every guarantee — specialty, bonus, training seat — in writing in the contract before you sign.** Verbal assurances are unenforceable.

## 4. Delayed Entry Program (DEP)

Source: DoDI 1332.14, girightshotline.org, operationmilitarykids.org, centeronconscience.org (retrieved 2026-07-12):
- **What it is:** After enlisting, most recruits enter the DEP (Army calls it the **Future Soldier Program**; Marines call members **poolees**). DEP members are enlisted into the **inactive/non-drilling Reserve**, are **unpaid**, receive **no benefits**, and are **not subject to the UCMJ**. Time in DEP counts toward the 8-year Military Service Obligation.
- **Length:** up to **365 days** (regulatory limit). Length depends on when your job/school/qualifications align.
- **Obligations:** stay out of legal trouble, maintain fitness/weight, keep the recruiter informed, and not use drugs; a positive drug test or arrest can cancel the contract.
- **Leaving DEP / changing your mind:** DEP is the easiest phase to exit. Per DoDI 1332.14, a member may request separation in writing to the recruiting station commander (an **entry-level separation** — no discharge characterization, generally an RE code allowing later re-entry). Recruiters cannot lawfully threaten, coerce, or claim you'll be jailed or "dishonorably discharged." Consequence: re-enlisting in the **same** branch later may require a waiver and you may lose your prior job/ship-date guarantee. National Guard members can, in rare states, face state-level consequences for not reporting (UNVERIFIED which states).
- **DEP bonuses/incentives:** the DEP itself pays nothing; enlistment **bonuses, referral incentives, and advanced rank for referrals** are written into the contract and paid after entering active duty. Advanced pay grade (up to E-2/E-3/E-4) is available for college credits, JROTC, Eagle Scout/Gold Award, or referrals depending on branch.

## 5. Waivers

Source: DoDI 6130.03, 32 CFR §66.7, war.gov, military.com, militarytimes.com, defense.gov, clearancejobs.com (retrieved 2026-07-12):
- **Common categories:** medical, moral/criminal conduct, drug/alcohol history, tattoos, dependents (single parents or >2 dependents), age, education tier, prior service, and weight.
- **Process:** approval is **branch-specific and discretionary** — a "whole-person" review by the Secretary of the Military Department concerned (or delegated authority). Conduct waivers require the "who/what/when/where/why" plus character references (clergy, school, law enforcement). Drug waivers require a disqualification period to lapse first. Waivers are **not portable** — a denial by one service can be re-attempted at another. Some offenses (e.g., sexual offenses requiring registration) are **not waiverable**.
- **How common waivers are:** Dr. Katie Helland, DoD director of military accession policy, stated at an October 31, 2024 Pentagon briefing that "nearly 77% of youth between the ages of 17 and 24 need some type of waiver to qualify for military service" — meaning only about 23% qualify without one (per AUSA/additudemag.com). A 2023 DoD Inspector General review found 17% of recruits in 2022 received a waiver, up from 12% in 2013, and that 77% of the 54,206 waiver requests were granted — the Marine Corps approving 98% (7,955 of 8,124), the Navy 84%, the Army 69%, and the Air Force 65% (per Military.com, April 29 2025, citing the 2023 DoD IG review).
- **CURRENT posture (tightening):** On **July 22, 2025**, Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth issued "Medical Conditions Disqualifying for Accession," making conditions such as current congestive heart failure, current schizophrenia treatment, history of paraphilic disorders, multiple sclerosis, prior organ transplant, cystic fibrosis history, and a suicide attempt within 12 months **ineligible for a waiver**, and requiring service-secretary approval for a second tier (13 conditions ineligible; 13 requiring secretary approval). This built on a March 4, 2025 accession-waiver guidance memo. The posture is actively narrowing in 2025–2026 — verify before assuming a condition is waiverable.

## 6. Guard and Reserve as an Alternate Path

**Label caution:** Guard/Reserve **drill pay and part-time benefits are NOT interchangeable with active-duty compensation.** Where figures below are Guard/Reserve, they are labeled as such.

### The seven reserve components (and the Space Force exception)
Source: nationalguard.com, militaryprephub.com, battalionduty.com, spaceforce.mil (retrieved 2026-07-12):
- **DoD reserve components:** Army National Guard, Air National Guard, Army Reserve, Navy Reserve, Marine Corps Reserve, Air Force Reserve.
- **Coast Guard Reserve** — sits under **DHS**, not DoD (affects some benefit administration).
- **Space Force** — **has NO traditional reserve component.** Under the Space Force Personnel Management Act (signed December 2023), it uses a **single combined component**: Guardians serve on **"sustained duty" (full-time)** or **"not on sustained duty" (part-time)**, with an inactive-status pool akin to the IRR. Part-time Guardians ("Guardians on Non-Sustained Duty") must complete the equivalent of **at least 36 days/year (48 drill periods + 12 annual-training days)** for a "good year." The Space Force's April 23, 2026 announcement selected exactly **247 Air Force Reservists** as its first Guardians on Non-Sustained Duty; Chief of Space Operations Gen. B. Chance Saltzman called it "an important step in the evolution of our service" (spaceforce.mil/airandspaceforces.com, April 2026). This is explicitly **not** a Space Force Reserve, and there is **no Space National Guard**.

### Drill schedule — and why "one weekend a month, two weeks a year" oversimplifies
The classic model is one drill weekend/month (a weekend = **4 drill periods/UTAs**, since each day is two periods) plus a ~2-week Annual Training. In reality, schools, deployments, additional duty, and mobilizations mean the real commitment is frequently higher. Since 9/11, Guard and Reserve units have deployed extensively (e.g., Army National Guard brigade combat teams did multiple Iraq/Afghanistan rotations) — **do not assume Guard/Reserve means no deployment.**

### Pay (labeled Guard/Reserve)
Source: legalclarity.org, militaryprephub.com, vetcalc.org (retrieved 2026-07-12). Drill pay uses the **1/30th rule**: one drill period = 1/30 of the equivalent active-duty monthly base pay; a 4-period weekend = ~4/30. Illustrative 2026 figures (Guard/Reserve, before allowances): an **E-4 with <2 years earns ~$419 for a four-drill weekend**; an E-6 at the same experience ~$453; a typical drill weekend ranges roughly **$230–$540** by rank/longevity. These are **not** a living wage — drill pay is supplemental. When **mobilized/activated**, members receive **full active-duty pay and allowances**.

### Benefits (labeled Guard/Reserve vs. active)
Source: tricare.mil-referenced sources, va.gov, military.com, collegerecon.com (retrieved 2026-07-12):
- **Health care:** Active duty = **TRICARE Prime at no premium**. Guard/Reserve (not activated) = **TRICARE Reserve Select (TRS)**, a premium plan (~$50/month individual, ~$240/month family per militaryprephub.com — verify current rates on tricare.mil). Activated 30+ days → full active TRICARE; 180 days of TAMP coverage after qualifying separation.
- **GI Bill:** **Montgomery GI Bill–Selected Reserve (Chapter 1606)** pays a flat monthly stipend — **$493.00 per full month for full-time enrollment** (effective Oct 1 2025–Sep 30 2026, per VA.gov, last updated Nov 17 2025; 3/4-time $369.00, 1/2-time $246.00) — and requires a 6-year Selected Reserve obligation with **no deployment needed**. **Post-9/11 GI Bill (Chapter 33)** requires qualifying **Title 10 active duty (≥90 aggregate days)** and scales in tiers by cumulative active service to as high as 100% (full public in-state tuition, housing allowance, and up to $1,000/year books). Post-9/11 is far more generous but activation-gated; drills, annual training, and initial skills training do **not** count. You must pick one benefit.
- **Retirement:** Guard/Reserve earn **points** (1/drill, 1/active-duty day); a "good year" needs ≥50 points; a pension requires 20 qualifying years and normally **cannot be collected until age 60** (reducible by 90 days per 90 consecutive activation days, floor age 50). Active-duty retirees can collect immediately at 20 years.

### State-level tuition benefits (National Guard) — varies dramatically
**Do not present a single national number.** State education benefits for the National Guard are set by each state and range from nothing to full tuition waivers at state schools. Examples cited (source: militaryprephub.com, militarybenefitsassistant.com, retrieved 2026-07-12): several states (e.g., Texas, Florida) offer additional tuition support; many offer full state-university tuition waivers. **Methodology to verify:** check the specific state's National Guard education office / state veterans affairs office, because eligibility, dollar caps, and school lists differ by state and change yearly.

## 7. Officer Preparation Pathways

### Service academies
Source: whitehouse.gov service-academy-nomination page, senate.gov/house.gov nomination pages, asvabprogram.com, usna.edu-referenced material (retrieved 2026-07-12):

| Academy | Branch(es) commissioned | Nomination required? |
|---|---|---|
| USMA West Point (NY) | Army | Yes (Congressional/VP/Presidential/service-connected) |
| USNA Annapolis (MD) | Navy, Marine Corps | Yes |
| USAFA Colorado Springs (CO) | Air Force, **and Space Force** | Yes |
| USMMA Kings Point (NY) | Merchant Marine / all services (reserve commission) | Yes (by state apportionment) |
| USCGA New London (CT) | Coast Guard | **No nomination** — direct competitive admission |

**There is no separate Space Force academy** — Space Force commissions via USAFA and AFROTC. Nominations come from your two U.S. Senators, your U.S. Representative, and the VP/President (family-linked). Eligibility: U.S. citizen, unmarried, no dependents, ~17 but not yet 23 (25 for USMMA) by July 1 of entry year. **Timeline:** open a pre-candidate file spring of junior year; take SAT/ACT and the **Candidate Fitness Assessment (CFA)**; complete the **DoDMERB** medical exam by winter of senior year; congressional nomination deadlines fall roughly **October–November of senior year**; academy application and nomination paperwork due **January 31**; appointments notified by ~April 15; report ~late June/early July. Academies are tuition-free in exchange for a ~5-year active-duty obligation; prep schools (USMAPS, NAPS, USAFA Prep) bridge near-miss candidates.

### ROTC
Source: goarmy.com, todaysmilitary.com, militaryonesource.mil, collegedata.com (retrieved 2026-07-12):
- **Army ROTC** (largest; ~270+ host programs), **Navy ROTC** (includes Marine option), **Air Force ROTC** (**also commissions into Space Force**). Offered at 1,000–1,700+ schools.
- **Scholarship types:** 4-year (high-school applicants), and 2- and 3-year (in-college). Cover full tuition/fees **or** room and board, plus a monthly stipend and book allowance. Army ROTC national scholarship board application opens ~June and runs through the following March for the HS class, with review boards Oct/Jan/Mar (2026–2027 cycle per goarmy.com).
- **Obligation:** commonly **8 years total** (mix of active duty and Selected Reserve/Guard); non-scholarship cadets can typically exit before the end of sophomore year without obligation. Army ROTC eligibility: U.S. citizen, at least 17 and under 31 in year of commissioning.

### OCS / OTS (for college graduates)
Source: todaysmilitary.com, militaryprephub.com (retrieved 2026-07-12): Officer Candidate School (Army, Navy, Marine Corps, Coast Guard) / Officer Training School (Air Force, and Space Force via DAF) is for those who already hold a **bachelor's degree** and did not do ROTC. Length varies ~**9–17 weeks**. The Marine Corps **Platoon Leaders Course (PLC)** commissions Marine officers via summer training during college. **Direct commissions** exist for professionals (medicine, law, chaplaincy). Boards weigh GPA (recommended ≥3.0, competitive 3.2–3.5+), a fitness test, leadership history, letters, and a personal statement.

### Coast Guard's distinct officer pathways
Source: militaryonesource.mil, todaysmilitary.com, scholarshipsandgrants.us (retrieved 2026-07-12): Because the Coast Guard has **no ROTC**, its routes are (1) the **Coast Guard Academy (CGA)** — no nomination; (2) **OCS**; and (3) the **College Student Pre-Commissioning Initiative (CSPI)** — a scholarship (targeted to students at qualifying/minority-serving institutions) under which you enlist while in college, receive pay/tuition, and attend OCS on graduation. Additional fast tracks include the Direct Commission Selected School (DCSS, for VMI/Citadel/Norwich/Texas A&M etc.) and Maritime Academy Graduate programs.

### Officer physical/academic entry standards (high level)
All commissioning sources require a bachelor's (except academy/ROTC which grant it), U.S. citizenship, a qualifying fitness test (branch PT test or the academy CFA), a DoDMERB/MEPS medical clearance, and — for aviation/technical fields — additional tests (e.g., AFOQT for Air Force officers, not the ASVAB). Age ceilings vary by branch and program.

## 8. Prior-Service Pathway

Source: DoDI 1300.04, goarmy.com IST page, afpc.af.mil, mynavyhr.navy.mil, airforce.com/ways-to-serve/prior-service, govfacts.org, legalclarity.org (retrieved 2026-07-12):
- **Two distinct routes.** (1) **Inter-Service Transfer (IST)** — moving branches *while still under contract*, requiring a **DD Form 368 "Request for Conditional Release"** approved by your current branch; keeps service continuity (pay, rank, retirement credit). (2) **Prior-Service Enlistment** — finish your contract, separate (DD 214), then re-enlist in another branch as a prior-service applicant; simpler because no permission is needed.
- **Re-entry gates:** your **RE (reenlistment) code** and discharge characterization govern eligibility; **RE-1 + Honorable** is cleanest. Less-than-honorable discharges typically require waivers and command clearance.
- **Rank/pay protection:** under DoDI 1300.04, transfers generally preserve grade/date of rank and credit accrued service; the Army IST program (formerly "Blue to Green") keeps E-1–E-5 grade and date of rank.
- **Branch-specific reality (current):** rules are branch- and MOS-specific and shift with manning. The **Air Force general officer IST program is currently SUSPENDED** due to force management; the **Space Force actively uses IST** but with narrowly defined eligibility (specific space/intel/cyber AFSCs, or demonstrated space experience). The **Navy** brings prior-service from other branches mainly via the **OSVET (Other Service Veteran)** program and won't release out of shortage specialties. Prior-service applicants who never completed Army/USMC basic must attend Army BCT within 365 days of enlisting into the Guard/Reserve. **Bottom line: confirm current MOS/rating demand and waiver posture with a recruiter, because these rules change frequently.**

## Recommendations (staged, with thresholds that change them)

**Stage 1 — Before contacting a recruiter (16–20, or any prospective applicant):**
- Take a free full-length practice ASVAB (officialasvab.com, March2Success) to get a baseline AFQT and identify weak subtests. **Threshold:** if your practice AFQT is below 50, budget 4–8 weeks of focused study on the four AFQT subtests (AR, MK, WK, PC) before scheduling — a 50+ opens all six branches and most jobs; below 31 you are ineligible for any branch until you improve.
- Decide the branch by *mission and job*, not marketing. If you want a specific technical MOS/rating/AFSC, work backward to the composite/line score it requires and target those subtests, because AFQT alone won't get the job.
- Gather medical and legal records now (immunizations, surgeries, ADHD/asthma history, any arrests). Given the July 2025 tightening of medical waivers, honest early documentation is the single biggest determinant of whether you clear MEPS without delay.

**Stage 2 — Choosing a path:**
- **Want college paid + officer commission:** apply to service academies *and* ROTC scholarships in parallel in the spring of junior year; they are free options with the earliest deadlines (Oct–Jan of senior year). Coast Guard aspirants: CGA needs no nomination — apply directly.
- **Want to serve part-time while keeping civilian life/school:** compare National Guard vs. Reserve on *state tuition benefits* (Guard) and *unit location/MOS availability*. **Threshold:** if your state offers a full Guard tuition waiver, the Guard is usually the stronger student deal; if not, weigh the Reserve's federal benefits.
- **Space Force interest:** note there is no part-time entry pipeline for new civilians yet (the single-component part-time model is currently filled by Air Force Reserve transfers) — plan on active ("sustained") duty or an AFROTC/USAFA officer route.

**Stage 3 — At MEPS and signing:**
- Get **everything** — job, bonus, training seat, ship date — written into the DD Form 4 and annexes. If it's not in the contract, it doesn't exist. If your qualified job isn't available in your window, DEP is preferable to accepting an open-needs contract you don't want.
- If you have second thoughts during DEP, you may separate in writing with minimal consequence — do it early and in writing to the station commander, not verbally to the recruiter.

**Benchmarks that should change your plan:** an AFQT jump across a category line (e.g., 49→50, or into 65+) materially expands job and bonus options and is usually worth a retake; a disqualifying medical finding should trigger a waiver conversation with the recruiter *and* consideration of a different branch (waivers are branch-specific); and any recruiter pressure to sign an unwritten promise is a signal to slow down.

## Caveats
- **Policy is changing fast in 2025–2026.** Fitness tests (Army AFT, Air Force/Space Force twice-yearly and 2-mile-run transition, Navy dual cycles, Marine sex-neutral combat PFT, Coast Guard new PFT) and medical-waiver rules are all mid-transition. Every number here should be reconfirmed against the current official publication before it drives a decision.
- **Coast Guard and Space Force are structurally different** and were not forced into parity: the Coast Guard is under DHS (affecting benefits/process), and the Space Force has no reserve component and a genuinely narrower, newer program set.
- **Guard/Reserve compensation is not active-duty compensation** — figures are labeled and should not be summed as if equivalent.
- **State-specific and specialty-specific figures vary** and are not reducible to a single national number; use the stated verification methodology.
- Secondary/test-prep sites were used only to corroborate or to surface conflicts, never as the sole source for a standard.
