# transition-pathway.md — Service-to-Civilian Transition Pathway

*A neutral factual reference on leaving the military: SkillBridge, TAP, the DD-214, VA disability, and VR&E. Current as of retrieval date 2026-07-12. Every figure below carries a named source and retrieval date; unconfirmed items are marked UNVERIFIED. This is not recruiting material, and it does not promise seamless credential transfer where none exists.*

---

## TL;DR

- **SkillBridge is not automatic.** Eligible service members can spend up to 180 days of their final active-duty window in an approved civilian internship while still drawing full military pay — but a unit commander has to approve it, can say no based on unit mission needs, and there is no appeal that overrides that discretion. Treat "I'm eligible" and "I'm approved" as two different facts.
- **TAP is mandatory, not optional**, for every separating or retiring service member with 180+ continuous days on active duty (this includes Guard/Reserve). It must start no later than 365 days before separation and ends in a Capstone review against Career Readiness Standards.
- **The DD-214 is the single document nearly everything else keys off of.** Its "character of service" line (Block 24) gates VA disability, VA healthcare, GI Bill, home loan eligibility, hiring preference, and state benefits — all of it, before any other criterion is even checked.
- **File your VA disability claim before you separate, not after**, using the Benefits Delivery at Discharge (BDD) program (180–90 days out). It's meaningfully faster than filing once you're already a civilian.
- **The credential gap is real and it is the most important thing on this page.** Military training builds real skill. It frequently does *not* build the civilian paperwork — the license, the certification, the academy graduation — that the civilian labor market legally requires to do the same job. Six examples below show exactly where that gap sits and how to close it.

## 1. DoD SkillBridge — the reality behind the headline number

SkillBridge lets an eligible service member spend up to 180 days of their last stretch of active duty working with an approved civilian company — an internship, apprenticeship, or training program — while the military keeps paying them (militaryonesource.mil, retrieved 2026-07-12). The employer doesn't pay a salary during this period; the service member is still on the military's payroll.

**The part that gets left out of most recruiting-adjacent explainers: the unit commander has to approve it, and can say no.** Per militaryonesource.mil, participation requires "approval from their unit commander." A secondary source (skillbridge.mil/faq, confirmed only via search synthesis — the direct fetch failed on a certificate error, so treat the specific rank threshold as UNVERIFIED) frames the approval authority as the first field-grade commander in the chain of command, typically an O-4 or above, who can approve or disapprove "based upon unit mission requirements." In plain terms: if the unit is short-staffed, deploying, or otherwise needs the service member present, a commander can deny SkillBridge even to someone who checks every eligibility box on paper. This is the actual gate — not the 180-day window, not the paperwork, the commander's judgment call about whether the unit can absorb the loss.

**What this means practically for someone planning a transition**: start the conversation with your chain of command early, well before the 180-day window opens, and don't treat SkillBridge as guaranteed just because you meet the stated eligibility criteria.

## 2. TAP (Transition Assistance Program) — mandatory, and it has a real deadline

TAP is the mandatory pre-separation curriculum. Per tapevents.mil and va.gov (retrieved 2026-07-12), it applies to essentially everyone separating or retiring from active duty, including Guard/Reserve members with 180+ continuous days on active duty. It is not something you can skip by being busy or by planning to figure it out after you're out — DoD policy requires it to begin no later than 365 days before separation.

The components run from an initial counseling session through a Capstone review that confirms the service member has met "Career Readiness Standards" — measurable benchmarks DoD considers evidence you're actually prepared to transition, not just that you sat through the briefings. In between, there's a mandatory one-day VA Benefits and Services course, a Department of Labor employment workshop (resume, interview, job-search fundamentals), and optional two-day tracks depending on your post-service goal (higher education, vocational/technical training, or entrepreneurship via Boots to Business).

**It depends**: the "ideal" earlier start times you'll see cited (roughly a year out for separating members, two years out for retirees) are recommendations, not the hard deadline. The hard deadline — the one that's actually enforced — is 365 days before separation. If your timeline gets compressed for any reason (early separation orders, medical board, etc.), the requirement is to start TAP as soon as possible within whatever time remains, not to skip it.

## 3. The DD-214 — why it matters more than any other single document

The DD-214 (Certificate of Release or Discharge from Active Duty) is the record of your service that essentially every other institution — the VA, state governments, civilian employers claiming veteran hiring credit, county veteran-benefit offices — will ask to see before granting anything. Per archives.gov and military.com (retrieved 2026-07-12), it governs VA disability compensation eligibility, VA healthcare enrollment and priority group, GI Bill and other education-benefit eligibility, VA home loan Certificate of Eligibility, federal hiring preference, state-level veteran benefits, and burial benefits.

**The line that matters most is Block 24 — character of service.** It reads one of five ways: Honorable, General (Under Honorable Conditions), Other Than Honorable, Bad Conduct Discharge, or Dishonorable. That single line is checked before any other eligibility criterion for most of the benefits above. Losing the physical document doesn't lose the benefit — certified copies remain obtainable for life through the National Archives — but a service member should know exactly what their Block 24 says well before they need to use it for something.

## 4. VA disability — how ratings actually combine, and why specialty matters

VA disability compensation rates each service-connected condition from 0% to 100% in 10% increments. **The ratings do not add.** VA uses a combined-ratings table where each additional condition is applied against your *remaining* non-disabled percentage, then the result rounds to the nearest 10%. Per va.gov (retrieved 2026-07-12), a 50% rating combined with a 30% rating produces a combined value of 65 — which rounds to a 70% combined rating, not 80%. This surprises a lot of veterans doing the math themselves for the first time.

### Specialty-linked common claims — this is not one-size-fits-all

Different jobs produce different injuries, and the claims data reflects that:

- **Artillery, armor, and other crew-served-weapons combat arms**: tinnitus and bilateral hearing loss dominate, driven by sustained proximity to large-caliber weapons fire without consistent hearing protection use in the field. Tinnitus is VA's single most-compensated condition nationally — more than 3.5 million veterans were rated for it as of 2025.
- **Aviation — aircrew and ground crew (flightline mechanics, ordnance, fuelers)**: tinnitus and hearing loss again, from chronic flightline/engine-run-up noise, plus cervical and lumbar spine conditions for aircrew from sustained vibration and G-loading.
- **Infantry and other dismounted combat arms**: knee, lower-back, foot (plantar fasciitis), and shoulder conditions — the cumulative toll of rucking, airborne/air-assault operations, and repetitive high-impact movement over a career.
- **Anyone deployed to a PACT Act–covered location** (Southwest Asia theater, Afghanistan, and others — not limited to a single specialty): asthma, chronic bronchitis, sinusitis/rhinitis, COPD, and certain respiratory cancers, now presumptively connected to burn-pit and airborne-hazard exposure under the 2022 PACT Act. A veteran who meets the service-era and location criteria doesn't have to individually prove burn-pit exposure caused the condition — the presumption carries that burden.

### File before you separate

Use the **Benefits Delivery at Discharge (BDD)** program: file a pre-discharge claim between 180 and 90 days before your separation date while still on active duty (Guard/Reserve on full-time active duty also qualify), and be available for VA exams within 45 days of filing. VA reviews service treatment records and schedules exams before you separate, targeting a decision within roughly 30 days *after* separation — noticeably faster than filing the standard way once you're already a civilian (va.gov, retrieved 2026-07-12).

## 5. VR&E / Chapter 31 — Veteran Readiness and Employment

VR&E is a disability-linked vocational rehabilitation benefit, distinct from the GI Bill. Eligibility generally requires a service-connected disability rating of 20% or greater that creates an "employment handicap," filed within a 12-year basic period of eligibility (roughly from date of separation or first VA rating). Veterans below 20%, or outside that window, may still qualify if VA finds a "serious employment handicap" (va.gov, retrieved 2026-07-12).

VR&E pays tuition/training costs plus a monthly subsistence allowance — the FY2026 maximum monthly rate, effective 10/1/2025, is $3,439.23, scaled by enrollment intensity and dependents. Participants can elect the (often higher) GI Bill subsistence rate instead if they qualify for it.

**It depends** — the comparison to the GI Bill that matters most: VR&E offers up to 48 months of entitlement versus 36 under the Post-9/11 GI Bill alone, and critically, VR&E draws from a *separate* entitlement bucket than the Post-9/11 GI Bill. Using VR&E first does not reduce your GI Bill months. A disabled veteran can complete a VR&E-funded program and still have a full GI Bill available afterward.

## 6. The Credential Gap — the part every crosswalk on this site has to name honestly

This is the centerpiece, not a footnote: **military training frequently does not confer the civilian license.** A service member can be genuinely excellent at a job in uniform and still be legally unqualified to do the civilian version of that job the day they take it off — because the civilian version requires a specific exam, license, or academy credential that military training builds toward but does not itself grant.

Six examples, chosen to span different career families so the pattern is visible rather than a one-off:

| Military role | Civilian credential needed | What the military gives you | What it does NOT give you | How to close it |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Combat Medic / Hospital Corpsman (68W, HM, 4N0X1) | State EMT/Paramedic license via NREMT | Deep trauma and battlefield medicine experience | Automatic NREMT certification — the Registry doesn't accept a military transcript as equivalent | Direct-sit NREMT exams where eligible; bridge courses (40–80 hrs for AEMT; accelerated tracks cut Paramedic programs 30–50%); then a separate state license application |
| Military Police / Security Forces (31B, USMC MP) | State POST certification / academy graduation | Patrol, use of force, firearms, defensive tactics, investigations experience | Automatic state certification — set state-by-state, and most states still require some or all academy hours even for prior MPs | Check the specific state POST agency's reciprocity rules; expect an abbreviated or full academy |
| Aircraft maintenance (15-series, AD/AM, 2A-series) | FAA Airframe & Powerplant (A&P) certificate | Substantial hands-on work on military airframes/engines | Automatic A&P eligibility — FAA counts only actual working time (not training time) and evaluates each case individually; written/oral/practical exams still required | JSAMTCC process + branch COOL Gap Analysis to map covered vs. self-study areas; document verified OJT hours; schedule FAA exams |
| Motor transport / heavy-vehicle operator (88M, 3531, 2T1) | State Commercial Driver's License (CDL) | Verified experience on commercial-equivalent military vehicles | An automatic CDL — the waiver only excuses the road skills test, not the written test or endorsements | FMCSA Military Skills Test Waiver within 1 year of leaving the role; pass the state written test |
| Cyber operations / IT (17C, 25-series, Cyber Warfare Ops) | Vendor-neutral certs (CompTIA Security+, CySA+) | Operational network-defense experience, often an active clearance | Automatic industry certification — operational experience isn't the same as a passed proctored exam, and many postings require the paper cert regardless | Branch COOL + Credentialing Assistance funding to sit exams pre-separation; use the gap-analysis tool per MOS |
| Combat Engineer (12B) and similar non-technical "engineer" roles | State Professional Engineer (PE) license | Construction, demolition, route-clearance, field-engineering experience | PE-qualifying experience in most cases — state boards generally don't count "combat engineer" work as true engineering experience; non-technical time may even be pro-rated out of roles that do count | ABET-accredited degree, pass the FE exam, document genuinely technical engineering work under a licensed PE, then sit the state PE exam |

**One source note on that last row**: the specific framing that "combat engineer work generally doesn't count toward PE experience" traces to a commercial test-prep vendor (ppi2pass.com), not an official state licensing board or NCEES statement. It's directionally consistent with how state boards describe pro-rating non-technical work, but it isn't primary-sourced — verify against the specific state board before treating it as settled. This is flagged in `unverified` in the companion JSON.

### The tool that makes the gap specific instead of vague: COOL

DoD's Credentialing Opportunities On-Line program (COOL — separately administered per branch as Army COOL, Navy COOL, Marine Corps COOL, and Air Force COOL, aggregated at cool.osd.mil) is the authoritative source for which civilian certifications a given MOS/rating/AFSC is eligible to pursue, whether the branch will fund the exam through Credentialing Assistance, and — most useful here — publishes per-credential **Gap Analysis** documents that name exactly which knowledge and skill areas military training does *not* cover for that specific credential. That's the credential gap made concrete instead of a general disclaimer: for a given job and a given certification, COOL will tell you precisely what's missing.
